Hello! My name is Ecce Djogbenou epse Houenou, and I am part of Team ML6 from the cohort 8 of Data Sciences Institute (DSI). In this team project, we applied machine learning techniques to predict diseases based on patient symptoms. The dataset contains 4,920 patient records with 132 binary symptom features and a target variable, prognosis, which includes 41 unique disease classes. The dataset was sourced from Kaggle.
My contribution to this project focused on exploratory data analysis (EDA), building and evaluating two of the six machine learning models: Random Forest and Bernoulli Naive Bayes. These models were chosen for their suitability in handling high-dimensional, binary data and for their interpretability. I also reviewed and contributed to my team members’ work, actively participated in team meetings to track progress and timelines, and assisted with compiling and integrating all team members’ contributions into the final README file.
After initial data cleaning, including the removal of an unnecessary column, I performed exploratory data analysis to understand the dataset’s structure. This included checking for missing values, examining class balance across diseases, identifying the top 15 most frequent symptoms, visualizing the distribution of total symptoms per patient, and analyzing average symptom counts by disease. These steps confirmed that the dataset is clean, and the disease classes are well-balanced. Multiple visualizations were produced to support these insights.
For predictive modeling, I applied Random Forest and Bernoulli Naive Bayes. Random Forest was used to capture potential interactions between symptoms, while Bernoulli Naive Bayes is well-suited for binary features. Both models achieved perfect classification performance, with 100% accuracy, precision, recall, and F1-score, indicating that the symptom patterns in this dataset are sufficient to distinguish all 41 diseases. Feature importance analysis from Random Forest highlighted the top 20 symptoms contributing to predictions, and confusion matrices for both models confirmed that all disease classes were correctly classified. The Bernoulli Naive Bayes model demonstrated that the independence assumption did not negatively impact performance, while Random Forest showed that modeling complex feature interactions did not improve classification in this deterministic dataset.
One challenge I faced was the synthetic nature of the dataset and the high number of duplicate records. Through discussions with team members and support from our learning support, I was able to overcome this challenge, allowing the team to complete the project successfully.
If I had additional time, I would test all six models on real-world clinical datasets, where symptoms often overlap across diseases, patients may report inconsistently, and data may contain noise or missing values.
Overall, this project was a great learning experience. I improved my GitHub workflow skills, collaborated effectively with my team, and had very engaging discussions about machine learning methods and medical data analysis.
